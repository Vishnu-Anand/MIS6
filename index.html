Chapter 6 Study Guide: Moore’s Law and More

This chapter covers the physical "engine" of the modern economy. We aren't just looking at chips and wires; we are looking at how the shrinking size and cost of hardware fundamentally change how businesses compete, plan, and manage environmental impact.

1. The Core Engine: Moore’s Law & Microprocessors

Moore’s Law: An economic observation by Gordon Moore (co-founder of Intel) that the number of transistors on a chip doubles roughly every two years.

  • The Business Impact: Computing power doubles for the same price, or the price halves for the same power. This predictability allows managers to plan for future capabilities that aren't technically possible today.
  • Microprocessor: Often called the CPU (Central Processing Unit). The "brain" of the device that executes software instructions.

2. The "Data Kitchen" Analogy

Distinguish between processing, memory, and storage by imagining a professional kitchen:

  • The CPU (Microprocessor) = The Chef. The faster the chef, the faster the "data meal" is prepared.
  • RAM (Volatile Memory) = The Counter Space. The immediate workspace. If the counter is tiny, the chef must constantly run to the pantry, slowing down production. Volatile means when the power is cut, the counter is wiped clean.
  • Hard Drive/Flash (Non-Volatile Storage) = The Pantry. Long-term storage. Non-volatile means items remain even without power.
[Source]

3. Konana’s Model of the Software Ecosystem

Hardware sits at the bottom of a "stack." This interdependence means a change in Layer 1 (Hardware) can force a company to upgrade its entire software suite.

  • Layer 1: Hardware: The physical foundation (CPU, RAM).
  • Layer 2: Operating System (OS): The "Manager" (Windows, iOS). Controls hardware and interacts with the user.
  • Layer 3: Database: Where information is organized and stored.
  • Layer 4: Middleware: The "Glue" allowing different software programs to communicate.
  • Layer 5: Application Software: The "User Layer" (Spotify, Excel).

4. Economics: Price Elasticity & The IoT

Price Elasticity of Demand measures how demand shifts when price changes. Tech is highly elastic.

  • Internet of Things (IoT): Low-cost sensors and processors embedded in everyday objects (lightbulbs, jet engines) to communicate data.
  • The Disney MagicBand: A $1 billion IoT investment. The real value is predictive analytics—knowing when to redirect staff to crowded areas to maximize guest satisfaction and spending.

5. The Environmental Cost: eWaste

eWaste: Discarded electronic devices containing toxic materials like lead, cadmium, and mercury.

Managerial Concern: Firms face "reputational risk" and regulatory fines when hardware ends up in open-air burning pits in developing nations.
