Chapter 6: Moore’s Law & More

Think of this chapter as the "Physical Foundation." Everything in business—from TikTok trends to AI trading—depends on the fact that hardware gets faster and cheaper at a predictable rate. If this engine stops, the software world stalls.

1. The Engine: Moore’s Law

Back in 1965, Gordon Moore noticed that we could fit twice as many transistors on a chip every two years.

Why should you care? It’s a "Manager’s Cheat Code." It means that if a project is too expensive or slow today, it might be perfectly viable in 24 months. It’s why your smartphone is more powerful than the computers that sent humans to the moon.

🍳 The "Data Kitchen" Analogy

If you want to understand how a computer actually works, imagine a kitchen:

  • The CPU (Chef): The one doing the actual work. A "faster" CPU is just a faster chef.
  • RAM (Counter Space): This is Volatile. It’s where the chef preps the meal. If the counter is small, the chef is slow because they can only work on one thing at a time. Turn off the lights (power), and the counter is wiped clean.
  • Storage (The Pantry): This is Non-Volatile. It’s where the ingredients stay long-term. Even if you go home for the night, the food stays in the pantry.

2. Konana’s Software Ecosystem

Software isn't just one "thing." It’s a layered stack. If you change the bottom layer, the top layers might break. This is why IT upgrades are so stressful for companies.

Layer 5: Applications (The apps you use, like Excel or Spotify)
Layer 4: Middleware (The "glue" that lets apps talk to each other)
Layer 3: Database (The organized digital filing cabinet)
Layer 2: Operating System (The boss; Windows/macOS)
Layer 1: Hardware (The physical chips and wires)

3. The IoT & "Price Elasticity"

In economics, Price Elasticity means: "If the price drops, will people buy way more?"

For tech, the answer is a massive YES. Because chips became so cheap (Moore's Law), we started putting them in lightbulbs, heart monitors, and dog collars. This is the Internet of Things (IoT).

The Disney Example: Disney’s MagicBands are an IoT masterpiece. They aren't just for opening doors; they allow Disney to track crowd flow in real-time. If a line gets too long, they can send a parade or a character to a different area to move the crowd. That's data-driven management.

4. The Dark Side: eWaste

Since we get new, faster phones every two years, the old ones have to go somewhere. eWaste is a massive environmental crisis. These devices are full of toxic heavy metals.

Managerial Tip: Companies that don't recycle properly face huge "reputational risk." Nobody wants to see their company logo on a laptop being burned in a toxic pit on the news.
