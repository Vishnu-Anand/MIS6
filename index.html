Chapter 6: Moore’s Law & Hardware

This chapter explores how Exponential Growth in hardware creates massive disruptive opportunities for managers[cite: 13, 51]. While we are hitting the physical limits of shrinking transistors, AI capabilities are now doubling every 6 months[cite: 35, 36].

1. The Core Concepts

  • Moore’s Law: Not a law of physics, but a business plan observation that the number of transistors on a chip doubles every 18 months[cite: 21, 26].
  • Semiconductors: Materials that conduct electricity under certain conditions and inhibit it under others[cite: 90]. They are the foundation of modern electronics[cite: 93].
  • The Fab: A multi-billion dollar facility used to manufacture chips. A single plant can cost $20+ billion and requires massive, ultra-purified water infrastructure[cite: 154, 156, 162].

🍳 How a Computer Works: The Data Kitchen

Think of your hardware as a workspace[cite: 75]:

  • CPU (The Chef): Runs the computer using software "recipes"[cite: 76, 81].
  • Main Memory (The Workspace): RAM is the immediate space where the chef works. It is Volatile (wipes clean when power is lost)[cite: 77, 79].
  • Storage (The Pantry): Where recipes and data are kept long-term. It is Non-Volatile[cite: 77, 80].
  • I/O Devices (Receiving/Shipping): How data enters or leaves the kitchen (keyboards, monitors)[cite: 78, 85].

2. Konana’s Software Ecosystem

Strategic decisions must consider ecosystem entrenchment. Because each layer depends on the ones below it, this creates "Lock-in" or high switching costs[cite: 61, 63, 70, 72].

3. Capacity vs. Transfer Speed

The Golden Rule: 8 bits = 1 byte[cite: 249].

  • Storage: Measured in Bytes (Capital B). Example: 1 Gigabyte (GB) is one billion bytes[cite: 134, 135, 250].
  • Transfer: Measured in bits (lowercase b). Internet speeds are Megabits per second (Mbps)[cite: 251, 252].
  • The "Gotcha": Your download speed in Megabytes will always look 8 times smaller than the Megabits your ISP promised[cite: 254, 255].

4. Bandwidth vs. Latency

  • Bandwidth: Think of this as the number of lanes on a highway. More lanes = more data at once[cite: 264, 265].
  • Latency: Think of this as the speed limit or delay. It is the time it takes for a single "car" to get from A to B[cite: 266].
  • Management Rule: Video streaming needs high bandwidth, but online gaming and Instapolls require low latency (speed of response)[cite: 280, 281, 282].

5. Disney’s Big Data Strategy

Disney used IoT (MagicBands) to solve complex operational problems[cite: 393]:

  • Operational Efficiency: They cut turnstile transaction time by 30%[cite: 431].
  • Staffing: Data tells them exactly how many costumed characters or staff are needed in different parts of the park[cite: 429, 430].
  • Capacity: They can now serve 3,000 to 5,000 additional guests per day because of efficient scheduling algorithms[cite: 423, 432].
