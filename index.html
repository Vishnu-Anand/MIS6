Chapter 6: Moore’s Law and Hardware

This chapter explores how exponential growth in hardware creates massive disruptive opportunities for managers. While we are hitting the physical limits of shrinking transistors, AI capabilities are now doubling every 6 months.

1. The Core Concepts

  • Moore’s Law: Not a law of physics, but a business plan observation that the number of transistors on a chip doubles every 18 months [source].
  • Semiconductors: Materials that conduct electricity under certain conditions and inhibit it under others. They are the foundation of modern electronics [source].
  • The Fab: A multi billion dollar facility used to manufacture chips. A single plant can cost 20 billion dollars and requires massive, ultra purified water infrastructure [source].

🍳 How a Computer Works: The Data Kitchen

Think of your hardware as a workspace:

  • CPU (The Chef): Runs the computer using software recipes.
  • Main Memory (The Workspace): RAM is the immediate space where the chef works. It is Volatile meaning it wipes clean when power is lost [source].
  • Storage (The Pantry): Where recipes and data are kept long term. It is Non Volatile [source].
  • I/O Devices (Receiving/Shipping): How data enters or leaves the kitchen such as keyboards and monitors.

2. Konana’s Software Ecosystem

Strategic decisions must consider ecosystem entrenchment. Because each layer depends on the ones beneath it, this creates lock in or high switching costs [source].

Layer 6: Consumer Applications (Apps you use daily)
Layer 5: Enterprise Applications (Large scale business software)
Layer 4: Middleware (The software that connects apps)
Layer 3: DBMS (The Database Management System)
Layer 2: Operating System (The hardware manager)
Layer 1: Hardware (The physical foundation)

3. Capacity vs. Transfer Speed

The Golden Rule: 8 bits = 1 byte.

  • Storage: Measured in Bytes with a Capital B. Example: 1 Gigabyte is one billion bytes [source].
  • Transfer: Measured in bits with a lowercase b. Internet speeds are Megabits per second [source].
  • The Gotcha: Your download speed in Megabytes will always look 8 times smaller than the Megabits your ISP promised.

4. Bandwidth vs. Latency

  • Bandwidth: Think of this as the number of lanes on a highway. More lanes equals more data at once [source].
  • Latency: Think of this as the speed limit or delay. It is the time it takes for a single car to get from A to B [source].
  • Management Rule: Video streaming needs high bandwidth, but online gaming and Instapolls require low latency.

5. Disney’s Big Data Strategy

Disney used IoT via MagicBands to solve complex operational problems:

  • Operational Efficiency: They cut turnstile transaction time by 30 percent [source].
  • Staffing: Data tells them exactly how many costumed characters or staff are needed in different parts of the park.
  • Capacity: They can serve 3,000 to 5,000 additional guests per day because of efficient scheduling algorithms.
